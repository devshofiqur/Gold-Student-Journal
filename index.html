Why Gold Has Been the World's Most Trusted Store of Value for Over 5,000 Years

AM
Ana Maria Oliveira
3rd Year Student · Published March 10, 2025 · 8 min read
Student Research

Gold bars stored in a central bank vault. Photo: Unsplash

Gold has captivated humanity for millennia. Long before modern financial systems existed, ancient civilizations recognized in this gleaming yellow metal a quality that few substances on Earth possess: permanence. Unlike iron, which rusts, or copper, which corrodes, gold does not react with air or water. It remains unchanged across centuries, even millennia. This chemical fact has profound financial implications.

The Chemistry of Permanence

Gold's atomic number is 79, placing it in Group 11 of the periodic table alongside silver and copper. What makes gold exceptional is its electronegativity and relativistic effects — the electrons in its outer shells move at speeds approaching the speed of light, contracting their orbits and making gold resistant to losing electrons (oxidizing). In practical terms: a gold coin buried in the ground for 3,000 years emerges looking almost identical to the day it was minted.

📚 Financial Vocabulary: A store of value is an asset that maintains its purchasing power over time. For an asset to qualify, it must be durable, divisible, portable, fungible, and scarce. Gold satisfies all five criteria naturally.

From the Pharaohs to the Federal Reserve

The earliest recorded gold currency dates to Lydia (modern Turkey) around 600 BCE. But gold's monetary role is far older — Egyptian pharaohs used gold weights as early as 3000 BCE. What's remarkable is the continuity: the same properties that made gold valuable to an ancient merchant in Alexandria make it valuable to a modern central bank in Frankfurt or Beijing.

"Gold is money. Everything else is credit." — J.P. Morgan, testimony to the United States Congress, 1912

The Modern Relevance

Today, central banks around the world hold over 35,000 tonnes of gold in reserve. In 2022 and 2023, central bank gold purchases hit multi-decade highs, driven largely by emerging market economies seeking to diversify away from the US dollar. This is not nostalgia — it is a calculated response to geopolitical and monetary risks.

For Brazilian students in particular, understanding gold is especially relevant. Brazil is one of the world's top-10 gold producers, with significant mining operations in Mato Grosso, Pará, and other states. The decisions made by global gold markets directly affect employment, environmental policy, and state revenues here at home.

Conclusion

Gold's 5,000-year track record as a store of value is not an accident of culture or myth — it is grounded in physical and chemical reality. In a world of floating currencies, digital assets, and monetary experimentation, gold continues to serve its ancient function: a reliable measure of value that outlasts the governments and ideologies that issue paper money.
